Visualizing Housing Data

Context

McGill School of Architecture wanted to visualize housing characteristics, specifically affordability, energy, and carbon, to foster a circular economy. When materials and buildings are used for as long as possible, waste is reduced, and sustainability is achieved creating a circular economy.

Client: McGill School of Architecture 
Project type: Data visualization website 
Team: 3 UX/UI Designers (including myself), 1 design lead, 1 homepage art director, 2 PMs, multiple engineers
Timeline: 4 months ~ 2022

The Problem

In order for homebuilders, financiers, and policymakers to make decisions that progress Canada towards a circular economy, robust building data is required. Unfortunately, building data is scattered across industries, lacks standardization, and is difficult to understand.


The Objective

Research, strategize, and design a web experience that provides organized, standardized, and comprehensible data visualizations about Canada’s housing characteristics. Key decision makers will be able to reference building data, and evaluate their own, to forge a sustainable housing future.

My Role

When I was onboarded, phase 1 of the project had already been completed meaning personas were defined, the general art direction had been established, and a handful of pages were already prototyped. Site requirements were also loosely outlined by the client. 

In collaboration with the team, I worked on several pages which involved designing the UX, UI, and animations. One of my project highlights included leading a discovery workshop to understand user needs for the housing map. During this phase, I worked with the client to identify what data was essential to include. In addition, I played a pivotal role in building out the design system which elevated the branding, ensured consistency across all design elements, and guaranteed an efficient prototyping process. Through my contributions, I was able to help the team create a successful and user-friendly product that exceeded client and user expectations. 

The Process

Discover & Define

We gathered and synthesized information from client whitepapers, mockups, and meetings which helped us create a suitable site architecture and structure page content.

Challenges

Small Budget

Pages were quickly prototyped and iterated upon after rounds of client feedback.

Challenging subject matter

We collaborated with the client to streamline information that was complex and academic in language, ensuring it was presented in a more accessible format for both ourselves and primary users.

Few websites to reference

The subject matter was niche, so we referenced design patterns from related industries such as real estate and architecture.

Develop & Deliver

The homepage effectively communicates the website’s purpose and features

Using stunning and interactive animations created by myself and another designer, we illustrated the capabilities of each feature, and redirected primary users to the most useful pages for them.

The housing map enables users to see building data at a city, neighbourhood, and property level

Data is broken down into scales, tables, and graphs, which makes comparing data simple. Depending on the filters applied, granularity of the data changes.

The housing passport presents building-specific data about affordability, energy performance, and carbon performance

This data can be used by decision makers to understand how different housing characteristics influence sustainability scores. 

Using principles of design pertaining to typography, colour, and layout, we designed a page template with a clear hierarchy making the data easy to scan. To avoid walls of text, info icons were used to explain uncommon terminology.

Material passports provide detailed data about a material’s impact on health and the environment

The page structure follows the same format as the housing passport so users don’t need to relearn a new page layout, and the data is just as scannable. 

The client requested a supplier map, which promotes buying locally, but budget constraints prevented us from building it. As a solution, we redirect users to prefilled Google Map searches, which offers better search functionality anyway. 

To help users find related materials, a carousel at the bottom of the page displays items based on related use case and material category.

With design scenarios, users can design their own buildings

Design up to 3 buildings simultaneously using a form and compare them side-by-side to understand their impact on global warming. Edit, delete, and add designs easily for an enjoyable and informative iterative process.

The glossary explains industry-specific terms that may be hard to understand from a web search   

The words are displayed in the form of dropdown buttons for effortless page scanning which reduces scrolling and visual overwhelm.

Styleguide

Outcomes

The website hasn’t launched yet, but during prototype testing, primary users unanimously praised the accessibility of the data sources. They found the website's greatest strength to be its dynamic nature, as it customizes data based on user inputs, offering personalized information for their housing projects. Finally, they loved the UI which sets it apart from other data-heavy academic websites.

Reflection

It’s important to handle client expectations early on to stay within budget. The client was ambitious and wanted a lot of features and functionality, but it was our job to explain why we should only design for user needs rather than wants resulting in the most impactful solution.
