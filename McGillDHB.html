Visualizing Housing Data

Context

McGill School of Architecture wanted to visualize housing characteristics, specifically affordability, energy, and carbon, to foster a circular economy. When materials and buildings are used for as long as possible, waste is reduced, and sustainability is achieved creating a circular economy.

Client: McGill School of Architecture 
Project type: Data visualization website 
Role: UX/UI Designer
Team: 2 UX/UI Designers, 1 design lead, 1 homepage art director, 2 PMs, multiple engineers
Timeline: 4 months ~ 2022

The Problem

In order for homebuilders, financiers, and policymakers to make decisions that progress Canada towards a circular economy, robust building data is required. Unfortunately, building data is scattered across industries, lacks standardization, and is difficult to understand.


The Objective

Research, strategize, and design a web experience that provides organized, standardized, and comprehensible data visualizations about Canada’s housing characteristics. Key decision makers will be able to reference building data, and evaluate their own, to forge a sustainable housing future.

My Role

When I was onboarded, phase 1 of the project had already been completed meaning the general art direction had been established, and a handful of mockups had already been made. Site requirements were also outlined by the client.

In collaboration with the team, I worked on several pages which involved designing the UX, UI, and animations. One of my project highlights included leading a discovery workshop to understand user needs for the housing map. During this phase, I worked with the client to identify what data was essential to include.

In addition, I played a pivotal role in building out the design system which modernized the branding, ensured consistency across all design elements, and guaranteed an efficient prototyping process. Through my contributions, I was able to help the team create a successful and user-friendly product that exceeded client and user expectations.

The Process

Discover & Define

Personas

Using the documents provided from the client, and findings gathered from a focus group conducted by our UX lead, I created three personas to better understand who we were designing for.

Goals

  • Empower users to make sustainable decisions for their own building projects.
  • Communicate which features are most useful for each user group.
  • Have the website serve as a benchmark for users from three different industries.
  • Make complex data easier to understand, compare, analyze, and share.

Challenges

  • Small budget: Pages were quickly prototyped and iterated upon after rounds of client feedback.
  • Challenging subject matter: We collaborated with the client to streamline information that was complex and academic in language, ensuring it was presented in a more accessible format for both ourselves and primary users.
  • Few websites to reference: The subject matter was niche, so we referenced design patterns from related industries such as real estate and architecture.

Site Architecture

With our three user groups in mind, we created a suitable site architecture with the aim of directing users to the right features and tools for them.

Develop & Deliver

Multiple pages were prototyped and delivered, however I’ll only highlight the ones I had a greater hand in creating.

The homepage effectively communicates the website’s purpose and features

The mockup from phase 1 was text-heavy, visually underwhelming and lacked CTAs. In phase 2, we use stunning and interactive animations created by myself and another designer to illustrate the capabilities of each feature. Using CTAs and active language, we redirect primary users to the most useful pages for them.

Please turn on your volume for all videos to listen to the rationale.

The housing map enables users to see building data at a city, neighbourhood, and property level

Key decision makers needed a way to efficiently compare and analyze up-to-date housing data, instead of getting lost in spreadsheets, so data is broken down into scales, tables, and graphs, which makes analysis easier. Depending on the filters applied, granularity of the data changes.

The housing passport presents building-specific data about affordability, energy performance, and carbon performance

Using principles of design pertaining to typography, colour, and layout, we designed a housing passport template with a clear hierarchy making the data easy to scan. To avoid walls of text, info icons explain uncommon terminology, and to reduce scrolling, users can navigate to different sections using the sticky navigation bar.

Users can explore building materials and compare their environmental impacts at a glance

Instead of aggregating material data from multiple sources across the web, users can quickly compare materials by the standardized ratings displayed on cards, and filters allow users to find alternatives based on use case and material category. Users can even download a spreadsheet of selected materials with the most relevant metrics to share with their team.

Material passports provide detailed data about a material’s impact on health and the environment

The page structure follows the same format as the housing passport so users don’t need to relearn a new page layout. To help users find related materials, a carousel at the bottom of the page displays items based on related use case and material category. 

The client requested a supplier map, which promotes buying materials locally, but budget constraints prevented us from building it. As a solution, we redirect users to prefilled Google Map searches, which offers better search functionality anyway.

With design scenarios, users can design their own buildings

Builders can design up to 3 buildings simultaneously using a form and compare them side-by-side to understand their impact on global warming. Edit, delete, and add designs easily for an enjoyable and informative iterative process.

The glossary explains industry-specific terms that may be hard to understand from a web search  

The glossary acts as a common point of reference for users from three different industries. The words are displayed in the form of dropdown buttons for effortless page scanning which reduces scrolling and visual overwhelm.

Visual System

Outcomes

We were unable to run usability tests due to budget constraints, but a feedback session where 13 users interacted with the prototype was conducted. Feedback was affinity mapped, and we found that there were no usability issues, and 100% of participants expressed positive feedback about the user experience and UI design.

What Our Users Had To Say

“The design of the website, and ease of interpretation, are current strengths.”

“Very visual in terms of results, which makes it easier to understand.”

“Educational, exposes concepts on circular economy/material ecology that are normally quite complex to learn about.”

“Concepts are explained in ways that are accessible to anyone.”

Reflection

It’s important to handle client expectations early on to stay within budget. The client was ambitious and wanted a lot of features and functionality, but it was our job to explain why we should only design for user needs rather than wants resulting in the most impactful solution.
