Visualizing Housing Data

Context

McGill School of Architecture wanted to visualize housing characteristics, specifically affordability, energy, and carbon, to foster a circular economy. When materials and buildings are used for as long as possible, waste is reduced, and sustainability is achieved creating a circular economy.

Client: McGill School of Architecture¬†
Project type: Data visualization website¬†
Team: 3 UX/UI Designers (including myself), 1 design lead, 1 homepage art director, 2 PMs, multiple engineers
Timeline: 4 months ~ 2022

The Problem

In order for homebuilders, financiers, and policymakers to make decisions that progress Canada towards a circular economy, robust building data is required. Unfortunately, building data is scattered across industries, lacks standardization, and is difficult to understand.


The Objective

Research, strategize, and design a web experience that provides organized, standardized, and comprehensible data visualizations about Canada‚Äôs housing characteristics. Key decision makers will be able to reference building data, and evaluate their own, to forge a sustainable housing future.

The Process

Discover & Define

We gathered and synthesized information from client whitepapers, mockups, and meetings which helped us create a suitable site architecture and structure page content.

Personas

Using the documents provided from the client, and findings gathered from a focus group conducted by our UX lead, I created three personas to better understand who we were designing for.

Goals

  • Empower users to make sustainable decisions for their own building projects.
  • Communicate which features are most useful for each user group.
  • Have the website serve as a benchmark for users from three different industries.
  • Make complex data easier to understand, compare, analyze, and share.

Develop & Deliver

Multiple pages were prototyped and delivered, however I‚Äôll only highlight the ones I had a greater hand in creating.

The homepage effectively communicates the website‚Äôs purpose and features

The mockup from phase 1 was text-heavy, visually underwhelming and lacked CTAs. In phase 2, we use stunning and interactive animations created by myself and another designer to illustrate the capabilities of each feature. Using CTAs and active language, we redirect primary users to the most useful pages for them.

The housing map enables users to see building data at a city, neighbourhood, and property level

Key decision makers needed a way to efficiently compare and analyze up-to-date housing data, instead of getting lost in spreadsheets, so data is broken down into scales, tables, and graphs, which makes analysis easier. Depending on the filters applied, granularity of the data changes.

The housing passport presents building-specific data about affordability, energy performance, and carbon performance

Using principles of design pertaining to typography, colour, and layout, we designed a housing passport template with a clear hierarchy making the data easy to scan. To avoid walls of text, info icons explain uncommon terminology, and to reduce scrolling, users can navigate to different sections using the sticky navigation bar.

Users can explore building materials and compare their environmental impacts at a glance

Instead of aggregating material data from multiple sources across the web, users can quickly compare materials by the standardized ratings displayed on cards, and filters allow users to find alternatives based on use case and material category. Users can even download a spreadsheet of selected materials with the most relevant metrics to share with their team.

Material passports provide detailed data about a material‚Äôs impact on health and the environment

The page structure follows the same format as the housing passport so users don‚Äôt need to relearn a new page layout. To help users find related materials, a carousel at the bottom of the page displays items based on related use case and material category.¬†

The client requested a supplier map, which promotes buying materials locally, but budget constraints prevented us from building it. As a solution, we redirect users to prefilled Google Map searches, which offers better search functionality anyway.

With design scenarios, users can design their own buildings

Builders can design up to 3 buildings simultaneously using a form and compare them side-by-side to understand their impact on global warming. Edit, delete, and add designs easily for an enjoyable and informative iterative process.

The glossary explains industry-specific terms that may be hard to understand from a web search ¬†

The glossary acts as a common point of reference for users from three different industries. The words are displayed in the form of dropdown buttons for effortless page scanning which reduces scrolling and visual overwhelm.

Styleguide

Outcomes

The website hasn‚Äôt launched yet, but during prototype testing, primary users unanimously praised the accessibility of the data sources. They found the website's greatest strength to be its dynamic nature, as it customizes data based on user inputs, offering personalized information for their housing projects. Finally, they loved the UI which sets it apart from other data-heavy academic websites.

Reflection

It‚Äôs important to handle client expectations early on to stay within budget. The client was ambitious and wanted a lot of features and functionality, but it was our job to explain why we should only design for user needs rather than wants resulting in the most impactful solution.
