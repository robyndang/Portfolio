Boosting User Engagement

Context

Weverse is a social media app that hosts multimedia content primarily from Kpop artists and fans. Weverse’s mission is to deepen artist and fan connections, regardless of language barriers, in the safety of their own online communities.

Client: Self-initiated project
Project type: Social media app
Team: Sole designer
Timeline: 2 months ~ Q4 2021

The Problem

Weverse, marketed as a solution to language barriers between Korean artists and fans, disappoints users with its hit-or-miss translation feature. Despite restricting posts and comments to community members, online harassment persists, lacking the expected positivity that would differentiate Weverse from other social media platforms. Moreover, low fan-to-fan engagement causes users to swiftly check in on artists and exit the app.


The Objective

Research, strategize, and design an experience that provides accurate translations, decreases online harassment, and increases fan-to-fan engagement making Weverse the must-have app for fans.

The Process

Discover & Define

Secondary Research

After analyzing 249 of Weverse’s top Apple app store reviews, I found reducing harassment was the most suggested feedback, followed by separating western artists from korean artists, and improving translations.

Surverying Users + Insights

With these suggestions in mind, I created a survey and distributed it into all fan communities on Weverse. Initially, few responded, but with the power of memes, I managed to get 161 responses. The following insights stood out to me.

28% don’t consider Weverse a social media app. They open the app just to see artist posts.

Checking artist posts and updates is the top reason users check Weverse, followed by responding to other fan posts.

34% think posting is pointless because of low engagement.

50% spend 15 minutes or less per day on the app.

63% joined 1-4 fan communities.

Users believe improving translations should be the top priority, followed by reducing harassment and adding dark mode.

Although 36-40% said they witnessed harassment of artists and fellow fans, only 6% reported being harassed themselves.

Empathy Map

Goals

An impact/effort matrix was used to determine goals.

  • Reduce online harassment.
  • Increase engagement to build strong fan communities.
  • Provide accurate and easy-to-access translations.
  • Make UI more intuitive and usable.

Develop & Deliver

Usability Testing

Multiple pages were prototyped for qualitative usability tests on two first-time users, six current users, and one previous user of Weverse resulting in the final features.

Reducing Online Harassment

Hide Sensitive Content

Before: Many users mentioned being reported then banned from Weverse after expressing a different opinion. Due to Weverse’s tedious appeals process, they weren’t able to get their accounts back. 

After: Instead of banning users, sensitive posts are hidden and only after reaching an even higher number of reports are users banned.

Block User

Before: 36-40% of survey respondents said they witnessed harassment of artists and fellow fans. 

After: This feature ensures users never have to interact with online bullies again. It allows them to curate what they wish to see without unnecessarily reporting other users. Please turn on your volume for all videos to listen to the rationale.

Language Filter

Before: Users from different cultures disagreed about what should be posted on community feeds leading to conflict that had to be addressed by the Weverse team. Additionally, users had to trouble themselves by sifting through posts they didn’t understand. 

After: I added a “Language” filter for community feeds.

Increasing engagement to build strong fan communities

Viewable Homepage Content

Before: Users can only view content on the homepage from communities they’ve already joined, making it useless as a promotional tool.

After: Users can view all content regardless of membership, but comments are restricted to community members only. This provides exposure to artists, while preserving the quality of users within each community.

Daily Question Poll

Before: The community feed is uninteresting because many users don’t know what to post.

After: Users can engage in community-wide discussions by participating in the daily question poll. Voting requires minimal effort, and users can also write responses and post to the feed. This daily activity motivates fans to regularly open the app, and there’s the added excitement of seeing what their artists voted for too.

View Followers

Before: Weverse uses subscriptions/subscribers instead of following/followers which is confusing for English speakers. Moreover, users can only see their subscriptions (who they're following).

After: The copy is changed to following/followers, and users can see their followers in case they wish to become mutuals increasing fan-to-fan engagement.

Providing accurate and easy-to-access translations

Add To List

Before: Users reported switching to Twitter to view fan translations whenever Weverse’s built-in translation feature was inadequate.

After: By allowing users to sort who they're following into lists, they can easily navigate to translator profiles in-app.

Following Page

Before: Users didn’t understand what the purpose of following other fans was since it seemed to have no effect on the posts they’re shown. 

After: Users can view posts from those they follow on the Following page. This way, they can view fan translations in a timely manner and deepen fan-to-fan connections. Knowing followers will see their posts will also encourage posting thoughtful content. 

Making the UI more intuitive and usable

Community

Before: Users were confused about how to switch between communities because the dropdown button looked like a verification checkmark. In addition, the profile pictures incorrectly implied users could click into individual artist profiles.

The UI of artist moments (stories) at the top of the community feed had text that was hard to read since they were on top of images, and the rectangle images didn't give users a good idea of what they'd be clicking into. Artist moments were also redundantly repeated in a different UI on the artist feed.

After: An image banner replaces the confusing multiple profile pictures, and one profile picture containing an artist’s logo is used to afford switching between artist communities.  

Only 16% of users reported using the Discover page often, so I removed it from the main navigation and placed a global community search at the top instead.

Artist moments only live on the artist feeds as it makes more sense there and allows room for the poll on the community feed. The UI was changed for better readibility and clarity.

Joining Communities

Before: There was no search functionality so users had to horizontally scroll through 30+ community cards. 

After: Users can filter between Korean and International artist communities, and search for specific names using the search bar.

Separate Notifications

Before: Whenever artists go on a reply spree, their notifications get lost among personal activity notifications. As we know, users mainly check Weverse to see artist posts and interactions with other users. 

After: I separated the notifications into Artist and My Activity. Only 12% of users reported joining more than 7 communities, so it isn't overwhelming to filter by community.

Outcomes

The ex-user participant said they would download Weverse again if these improvements were implemented.

All six of the current users prefer the redesign to the original. They believe it solves the issues they had with Weverse, and stated they’d spend more time on the app if improvements were applied.

Both first time users found the redesign intuitive to use overall, and believed the added features would make for a more engaging experience.

Reflection

Since this was my first ever product design project, I inevitably failed to consider some factors. If I could redo the usability tests, I would include a Net Promoter Score, instead of asking open ended questions to measure how much they liked the new features. In future projects, to have a clearer, and more quantifiable result of user experiences, I would choose an appropriate metric system in the testing phase.
